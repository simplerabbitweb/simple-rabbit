Your About page is the second most visited page on your website. Most service businesses are wasting it.

By Leann Frank · March 16, 2026 · 6 min read

The About page gets more traffic than almost any other page on a service business website. A potential client lands on your homepage, decides you might be relevant, and immediately clicks to About. They want to know who you are before they go any further.

That makes the About page one of the most important pages you have. Most service businesses are writing it like a résumé.

Not a bad résumé, necessarily. Just the wrong document for the job. A résumé is organized around credentials and chronology. What a high-paying client visiting your About page actually wants is the answer to a different set of questions altogether.

What high-paying clients are actually asking

When someone clicks to your About page, they are not looking for your professional history. They are not trying to understand how you got into your field or trace the arc of your career. They are running a fast internal check: is this the right person for my situation? Do I want to work with them? Can I trust them?

That check takes about thirty seconds. Most About pages fail it, not because they are badly written, but because they answer the wrong questions.

"I started this business because I had a passion for..." does not answer anything your client is actually asking. Your passion is your business. What they need to know is whether you can help them, whether you have done it for people like them, and whether working with you is going to be worth it.

The mistake runs deeper than just starting sentences the wrong way. It is a structural problem. Most About pages are organized around the founder's story, told in the order it happened. High-paying clients don't need your story. They need to know what your expertise means for them.

What the About page actually needs to do

Three things. Each one builds on the last.

Establish that you are the right expert for the specific person visiting. This is not a general credential statement. "Award-winning designer with ten years of experience" tells a high-paying client very little. "I work specifically with women-owned service businesses who are already doing excellent work but whose websites don't reflect it yet" tells them immediately whether they are in the right place.

The specificity is the point. When your About page describes your client exactly, the right person reads it and thinks: she is talking about me. That recognition is the foundation of trust, and it happens before they have read a single testimonial.

Create actual human connection. High-paying clients want to work with someone they like. This is not a soft or fuzzy idea. It is practical. High-end service relationships involve real communication, disagreements, revision rounds, and judgment calls. People hire someone they believe they can work with over the course of a project.

This is where the About page earns its value beyond just credibility. A photo that looks like you, not a stiff corporate pose. A sentence or two about how you actually think about the work, what you find most interesting about it, what kind of client relationship energizes you. Specificity here does more than polish. Something real is more convincing than something perfect.

Make the next step obvious. Most About pages end with nothing. The content stops and the visitor is left to figure out where to go. That is a missed opportunity at the exact moment they have decided they like what they see.

End your About page with a clear, specific call to action. Not a generic "feel free to reach out." Something that tells them what the next step looks like: "If this sounds like where you are, I would love to talk. Book a free call here." The reader is already warmed up. Give them somewhere to go.

The most common About page mistakes

Telling your story in chronological order. Your path to this work is interesting to you. To a potential client deciding whether to hire you, what matters is where you ended up and who you serve now. Start with your positioning, not your origin. If the origin story is genuinely relevant, bring it in after you have established your expertise and made clear who your work is for. Most clients will never read that far anyway, and the ones who do are already interested.

No photo, or the wrong photo. A professional photo on an About page is not vanity. People are deciding whether to trust a human being, and a clear image gives them something to anchor to. The wrong version is a tiny headshot in a corner, a photo from several years ago that no longer looks like you, or a group shot where the visitor has to guess which person you are.

The photo should look like the version of you who shows up on a discovery call. Natural, confident, clear. That is all it needs to do. It does not need to be a full studio shoot. It needs to be current and genuine.

Talking about the business instead of the client. Sentences that start with "We are a boutique studio dedicated to..." or "I founded this company with a mission to..." are organized around the business. The visitor already knows this is your business. What they need to hear is what your approach means for them and what working with you will actually be like.

Read each sentence on your About page and ask: does this tell my ideal client something useful about what they can expect? If the answer is no, rewrite or cut it.

A useful test

Read your About page as someone who has never heard of you. They found your website through a referral or a Google search. They are a serious buyer, evaluating two or three options. In thirty seconds on your About page, do they know:

  • Exactly who you serve?
  • What you are specifically good at?
  • What kind of person you are to work with?
  • What to do next?

If the answer to any of those is no, the page is not doing its job. And it is worth knowing that this is not primarily a writing problem. The best copy in the world does not help if the structure is wrong and the page is answering questions your clients are not asking.

What this looks like when it works

A well-built About page for a service business does not need to be long. Some of the most effective ones are under 300 words. What they have in common is structure: an opening that states clearly who you are and who you work with, a short description of your approach that communicates something real about how you think, a photo that looks like a person someone would trust, and a call to action at the end that tells the visitor exactly what happens next.

This page does what your homepage cannot always do. Your homepage needs to catch attention quickly and move a visitor deeper into the site. Your About page has a visitor who has already decided to learn more. They clicked. They are there. The job now is to confirm that they are in the right place, make them want to work with you specifically, and give them a clear path forward.

Most About pages do none of those things well. Fixing that one page, with clear positioning, genuine voice, and a direct call to action, often changes the conversion rate of the entire site.
